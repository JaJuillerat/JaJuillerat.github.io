Data for "Composite numbers that remain composite when inserting, replacing, or deleting a digit"

The data on this page is associated with the paper "Composite numbers that remain composite when inserting, replacing, or deleting a digit" by Ian Fultz, Jacob Juillerat, and Aidan Kuster. There are two main lists to aid with following the constructions in the paper:

(i) a list of positive integers b and the primes p for which the order of 10 modulo p is b, and

(ii) a list that contains the covering systems presented in the tables in the paper.

Details regarding these lists are written below.

(i) a list of positive integers b and the primes p for which the order of 10 modulo p is b.

Each element of this list is itself a list of the form [b,[p1,...,pt]]. The first entry b indicates that what follows is describing primes p for which the order of 10 modulo p is b. The b correspond to the moduli used in the covering systems in the paper. If pt-1 = pt, then the numbers p1,...,pt-2 are distinct primes p for which the order of 10 modulo p is b, and pt-1 = pt is a composite number which is not a prime power and which is relatively prime to the product of the primes p1,...,pt-2. Each prime divisor of pt is such that the order of 10 modulo that prime is equal to b. These primes are determined by factoring or partially factoring Phib(10), where Phib(x) is the b-th cyclotomic polynomial. The list [b,[p1,...,pt]] is indicating that there are at least t primes for which 10 has order b modulo p.

To reduce the overlap of data, this list only contains moduli that were not used in the paper "Consecutive primes which are widely digitally delicate". The list of positive integers b and the primes p for which the order of 10 modulo p is b used in the paper "Consecutive primes which are widely digitally delicate" can be found here.

(ii) a list that contains the covering systems presented in the tables in the paper.

This is a series of lists for x in {1, 3, 4, 6, 9} and d in {0, 3, 4, 6, 7, 9} of the form tablex[x] or tabled[d], respectively. Each list gives the information for the covering for the specified x or d value found in the tables in the paper. Each table is a list of the form [a, b, p] corresponding to the congruence k = a (mod b) where p is a prime (or composite, but then p is associated with a prime divisor of this composite number) and b is the order of 10 modulo p. In other words, p appears in one of the lists in (i) above for b. As above, p may appear twice (and hence b twice) if p is composite with at least two distinct prime factors.

If q1 and q2 are two distinct prime factors of p in this case, then they are prime factors of Phim(10) which are relatively prime to b and hence 10 has order b modulo each of the primes q1 and q2. Thus, in the case that p is composite, the interpretation of [a, b, p] is that the congruence represented is k = a (mod b) and the prime p is one of q1 and q1, where each of these primes can be used with a different value of a.
